📖 16x SelfHeal — User Guide

What is this?

A live sandbox that makes high availability concrete. It simulates a microservice system — load balancer, services, caches, databases, queues — and lets you break it on purpose. Watch traffic route around failures, circuit breakers trip, replicas get promoted, and the orchestrator spin up replacements. What you're seeing is exactly what Kubernetes, health checks and retry logic do for production systems.

The map

Each hexagon is a running instance. Particles are live requests flowing from the internet through the system. Colors: green healthy · amber degraded/overloaded · blue booting · red dead. The bar under each node is its load. Hover a node for live details; click a node to kill it. Scroll to zoom, drag to pan, double-click to re-fit.

Chaos controls

💥 Inject chaos kills a random instance. Targeted actions use the group selector: partition makes a group unreachable, latency and CPU spike degrade it, traffic ×3 simulates a surge, and 🐒 Chaos Monkey keeps breaking random things until you cage it. Scenario buttons run scripted multi-step incidents.

What self-healing looks like

After a crash, watch the sequence: (1) requests to the dead node fail and retry against healthy replicas — this brief error window is the detection gap; (2) health checks fail twice and the load balancer ejects the node from its pool; (3) the orchestrator schedules a replacement, which boots (blue) and rejoins; (4) the incident banner clears and MTTR is recorded. Kill a database primary to watch a replica get promoted via leader election. Partition a cache or DB and watch services serve degraded fallbacks instead of failing.

AI Copilot (OpenRouter)

Add an OpenRouter API key in Settings to unlock deep AI assistance — not just chat: the copilot sees the live topology, metrics and event log. It can score your architecture and apply fixes, write post-mortems from the real event timeline, turn plain English into executable chaos scenarios ("simulate Black Friday with a payment DB failure"), and narrate incidents in client-friendly words. When the AI proposes actions you'll get a ▶ Run button — nothing executes without your click.

Topology editing

Scale any group up or down with the +/− controls (watch the orchestrator do the work). Add new service groups with their own dependencies. Presets: Simple Web App, E-commerce Microservices, Event-Driven Pipeline.

Metrics

Success rate, throughput, average latency and healthy-pod counts update per second. MTTR (mean time to recovery) is measured from the first failure to full recovery — use it to compare architectures: does 2 replicas vs 4 change your recovery story?

Keyboard shortcuts

Space pause · K kill random · M chaos monkey · 1/2/3 presets · F fit view · ? this help

For internal use

Use it to sanity-check replica counts, visualize retry/fallback strategy, and rehearse incident narratives before touching production. It's a mental model gym — the same failure patterns apply to your real Kubernetes configs.
